# Suspect Deception and Red Herring Discipline v1

## Purpose

This document defines how AI_IF handles suspect deception, false statements, omissions, red herrings, alibis, and innocent suspect clearance.

Core rule:

> Suspects may lie. The Game Master may not lie.

A character may deceive the player in-character only if the deception is authored, bounded, and discoverable. The Game Master must preserve the canonical truth and provide fair ways for the player to test or challenge suspect statements.

## Suspect Deception Rule

Suspects may:

- lie directly;
- omit relevant facts;
- minimize their behavior;
- exaggerate another suspect's behavior;
- evade emotionally;
- tell a technically true but misleading statement;
- be honestly mistaken;
- protect someone else;
- hide shameful or embarrassing information unrelated to the crime.

Every deception must define:

- the false statement or omission;
- the hidden truth;
- why the suspect lies;
- what the suspect actually knows;
- what the suspect does not know;
- what evidence can expose or challenge the lie;
- whether the lie points to guilt, self-protection, shame, fear, or innocent misdirection;
- how the GM should answer follow-up questions;
- what happens when the player confronts the suspect with contradictory evidence.

## Game Master Truthfulness

The GM must not invent lies during runtime.

The GM must not silently change whether a suspect's statement is true in order to create a twist.

The GM must not use narrator text to falsely state canonical facts.

Allowed:

> Julian says he never touched the glass.

Not allowed:

> Julian never touched the glass.

In-character speech may be false. Canonical narration and case-board facts must remain truthful.

## Deception Discovery Path

Every lie, omission, or misleading statement must have at least one fair discovery path:

- physical evidence;
- document evidence;
- another suspect's testimony;
- contradiction with timeline;
- contradiction with alibi;
- contradiction with object placement;
- contradiction with known habit or routine;
- emotional inconsistency;
- repeated questioning after evidence is found.

If a lie cannot be challenged, exposed, or fairly interpreted, it should be removed or converted into a non-essential flavor detail.

## Alibi Claim vs Alibi Truth

For each suspect, the package should distinguish:

- alibi claim;
- alibi truth;
- what the suspect saw;
- what the suspect could not have seen;
- what the suspect is hiding;
- what clears or implicates the suspect.

A suspect's alibi may be false, incomplete, or misleading, but the package must define how the player can test it.

## Innocent Suspect Clearance Rule

Every non-culprit suspect must have a clearance path.

A clearance path should establish at least one of:

- lacked motive;
- lacked method;
- lacked opportunity;
- had a contradictory timeline;
- could not access the required object;
- had a false but innocent reason for lying;
- appears suspicious for a reason unrelated to the crime.

A non-culprit may remain morally compromised, ashamed, evasive, or guilty of a lesser wrongdoing, but the player must be able to understand why they are not responsible for the central mystery.

## Red Herring Fairness Rule

A red herring is fair only if it has:

- an apparent implication;
- an innocent or alternate explanation;
- a discovery path;
- a clearance path;
- a reason it seemed suspicious;
- a reason it does not satisfy the full proof chain.

Do not leave red herrings permanently unresolved unless the unresolved detail is explicitly irrelevant and not part of the central proof path.

## Bounded Suspicion

A red herring should create suspicion without becoming an unresolvable alternate solution.

The player should be able to say:

> I understand why this looked suspicious, and I understand why it does not prove the case.

## Culprit Deception

The culprit may lie more substantially than innocent suspects, but the lies must still be fair.

The culprit's lies should be exposed through:

- contradictions;
- physical evidence;
- timeline conflict;
- motive evidence;
- mistaken detail;
- over-specific denial;
- evidence connected to their personal object, habit, or opportunity.

The culprit should not confess merely because questioned. The GM should require proof confrontation before confession or final reveal.

## Innocent Lies

Innocent suspects may lie for human reasons:

- shame;
- fear of blame;
- protecting a relationship;
- concealing a lesser wrongdoing;
- embarrassment;
- loyalty;
- panic;
- grief;
- avoiding scandal.

The package must make the innocent motive for lying discoverable.

## Case-Board Treatment

Case-board entries should never label a suspect as lying unless the player has discovered the contradiction.

Use neutral phrasing:

- Known claim:
- Contradiction:
- Unresolved significance:
- Possible inconsistency:

Avoid:

- She is lying.
- His alibi is false.
- This proves he is guilty.

Use direct lie labels only when the player has earned that interpretation.

## Validation Requirements

Validator must check:

- each suspect has an alibi claim and alibi truth;
- each lie has a discovery or contradiction path;
- each red herring has a clearance path;
- each innocent suspect can be cleared;
- culprit deception is discoverable but not prematurely obvious;
- GM-facing text does not reveal lies as lies before the player earns it;
- no red herring remains unfairly unresolved.

## AI Playtest Requirements

AI playtester must test:

- trusting each suspect at face value;
- challenging each suspect's statement;
- accusing each innocent suspect;
- confronting the culprit before full proof;
- confronting the culprit after full proof;
- resolving each red herring;
- clearing each innocent suspect.
